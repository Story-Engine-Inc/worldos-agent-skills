# World design principles

Use this reference while turning a premise into a playable Simulation. It is design guidance, not an MCP schema.

## Start from a player fantasy

Describe the world in one sentence from the player’s point of view:

> You are **someone**, trying to **achieve something**, while **forces with agency** make that difficult.

Specify whether the player acts as an individual, household, organization, faction, or state. Keep that authority consistent. The world must not perform major player decisions on the player’s behalf.

## Build a causal loop

A strong loop contains:

1. new information, pressure, or opportunity;
2. a choice with at least two credible approaches;
3. an immediate consequence;
4. a persistent state change;
5. a new situation shaped by that change.

Avoid choices where one option is an obvious free reward. Trade time, money, trust, safety, reputation, political capital, or future flexibility against each other.

## Design signature situations

List several situations specific to the setting rather than relying on generic good/bad events. Each should reveal a recognizable institution, social norm, constraint, or darkly funny trade-off.

For each situation, define:

- who creates the pressure;
- what they want;
- what the player knows;
- two or three plausible responses;
- the cost of each response;
- which persistent facts can change;
- how the world may react later.

Use fictional people, organizations, products, and media unless the user has a legitimate reason and rights to use real entities. Do not download or embed protected promotional assets.

## Make the opening active

The first screen should answer:

- Where am I?
- Who am I?
- What just happened?
- What can I do now?
- Why does it matter?

Seed only enough content to create momentum:

- one strong story beat;
- one or two messages or posts when social apps matter;
- two or three player-voiced action suggestions;
- visible starting resources and risks;
- one immediate objective or deadline when appropriate.

Do not front-load future twists or a complete encyclopedia. Let hidden events emerge through play.

## Give non-player actors agency

Important characters and factions need goals, information, resources, constraints, and relationships. They should act between player decisions when the setting calls for it, but their actions must remain causally grounded in established state.

Distinguish character voices with priorities and tactics, not only surface mannerisms.

## Preserve difficulty

Treat a player declaration as an attempt, not an automatic result. Outcomes should reflect preparation, capability, opposition, uncertainty, and cost. Long-term achievements should advance through quests, time, training, alliances, investment, or other visible mechanisms.

Failure should create a changed situation rather than merely block the story. Use partial success, delay, exposure, debt, injury, lost trust, or new obligations when appropriate.

## Keep the interface focused

Every installed app should answer one of these questions:

- What happened?
- Who can I talk to?
- What is changing?
- What do I own or owe?
- Where is everyone?
- What must I decide next?
- How can I act?

If an app does not support the core loop, remove it. More windows do not automatically create more depth.
